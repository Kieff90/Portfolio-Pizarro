The context

An Italian startup had a clear hypothesis: Italian SMEs spend 4–6 hours per week on competitive intelligence — monitoring competitor pricing, product launches, and market positioning. Most do it manually via Google searches and spreadsheets. An AI-powered platform could reduce this to 15 minutes per week.

The founding team had strong domain knowledge (B2B SaaS for Italian SMEs) and technical capability (two senior engineers). What they lacked was a delivery process. The first version of the product had been "in development" for 6 months without a single user seeing it. The hypothesis had never been tested with real users.

The problem with the first 6 months

The team had been building in isolation — designing features based on what they thought users needed, without validation. By month 6, the product had a sophisticated backend and no users. The engineers were optimising a system whose core hypothesis had never been tested.

This is the most common failure mode in AI product development: technical teams build excellent solutions to problems that users don't actually have, in the way they imagined they had them.

The delivery approach

The first decision was to stop building and start learning. Before writing another line of feature code, we needed to validate three things: Is the problem real? Is our proposed solution the right one? Are users willing to pay for it?

Week 1–2
Discovery — 15 user interviews
Paused all development. Ran 15 structured interviews with Italian SME owners and operations managers. Mapped the actual competitive intelligence workflow: what they monitored, how often, what tools they used, what they did with the information. The findings invalidated 3 planned features and revealed 2 high-priority needs that weren't in scope.
Week 3–4
Prototype validation — 15 more interviews
Built a clickable prototype (no backend) and tested it with 15 additional users. Measured: do they understand what it does? Would they use it weekly? Would they pay for it? Iterated the UX twice based on feedback before writing a single line of production code.
Week 5–6
Sprint 1 — Core LLM pipeline
Built the minimum viable LLM pipeline: competitor monitoring (web scraping + Claude analysis), weekly digest generation, email delivery. No dashboard yet — just the core value in the simplest possible form. Tested with 5 beta users at end of sprint.
Week 7–8
Sprint 2 — Production + LLM governance
Production deployment with LLM output validation (hallucination checks, source verification), model versioning governance, and monitoring. Onboarded first 10 paying users. 150+ competitors analysed in the first month of operation.

The LLM governance layer

Competitive intelligence is a context where hallucination has direct business consequences. If the AI tells an SME that a competitor launched a product that doesn't exist, the SME might make a strategic decision based on false information.

We built three validation layers before any output reached a user:

  • Source verification: every claim in the AI-generated digest was anchored to a verifiable source URL. No source = no claim.
  • Confidence scoring: outputs below a confidence threshold were flagged for human review before sending.
  • Prompt regression testing: a suite of 40 test cases run against every model version update before deployment.

This governance layer added 3 days to the sprint — and saved the team from a significant trust problem with early users. According to research by Anthropic (2024), users who encounter a single hallucination in an AI tool have a 60% lower retention rate than users who never encounter one.

Why 2 months was possible

Speed came from discipline, not shortcuts. The 30 user interviews before building meant every sprint hour was spent on validated features. The prototype validation meant we didn't discover UX problems in production. The LLM governance layer meant we didn't spend the first month of operation fixing trust problems.

"The delivery structure Pedro brought gave us a way to move fast without building the wrong thing. The 30 interviews before we wrote code felt counterintuitive but saved us months." — Founder, AI Startup
